Executive Summary

1.
Site Intelligence welcomes the opportunity to respond to this statutory consultation on the draft Regulations and Guidance implementing planning committee reform under section 54 of the Planning and Infrastructure Act 2025. We broadly support the direction of travel: mandatory delegation for Tier A applications (Regulation 4, Schedule 1) and the introduction of a gateway test for Tier B referrals (Regulation 5, Schedule 2) represent a significant step toward a more efficient, consistent, and evidence-based planning system.
2.
However, we have a central concern. The gateway test under Regulation 5, as interpreted by Guidance Paragraphs 19–21, relies on subjective criteria — principally whether an application raises "significant planning matters having regard to the development plan" (Criterion A) or "economic, social or environmental significance to the local area" (Criterion B). In our experience, backed by analysis of 114,700 planning appeals over 10 years, this formulation is broad enough to perpetuate the very inconsistency and delay that the reform is designed to address. Guidance Para 21’s examples — including "a change of use of a community shop in a rural area" — set such a low threshold that Criterion B could capture the majority of non-trivial applications.
3.

4.
Key findings:
  • 40.9% of refused major residential applications are overturned on appeal (2,247 of 5,490 over 10 years) — nearly double the 23.1% rate for minor residential. This is the application category most affected by the Tier B gateway test under Regulation 5. The consistency of this pattern over a full decade eliminates any argument that it is a temporary aberration.
  • 749 committee decisions identified from 30,000 decision letters (91.8% coverage of all allowed appeals). Of those where the decision route was identifiable from the letter text, 89.7% were committee refusals and 10.3% were delegated. This confirms that committee refusals are disproportionately represented among overturned decisions.
  • 87 cases where committees refused against officer recommendation. In every case in our allowed-appeals dataset, the inspector sided with the professional officer. While our sample is drawn from allowed appeals (so we cannot calculate a national overturn rate for officer overrides from this data alone), the finding is consistent with Lichfields’ independent finding that 65% of all appeals succeed where committees refuse against officer recommendation ("Refused for Good Reason?", 2018).
  • 4,056 appeals in constrained locations were allowed — including 3,046 in the Green Belt, 733 involving listed buildings, 293 in AONB/National Landscapes, 90 in flood risk areas, and 64 affecting SSSIs. These constraints are precisely the factors that could trigger referral under the gateway test’s Criterion A — yet inspectors found refusals unjustified in thousands of cases.
  • 6,108 costs applications over 10 years, with 2,670 in allowed appeals. The estimated taxpayer burden is £66m–£166m. The worst-performing LPAs by costs exposure include Cornwall (87 awards), Barnet (44), Wiltshire (43), and Bromley (36). South Somerset has the highest costs rate at 13.6%.
  • The problem is getting worse: Committee determinations among overturned appeals have increased 28% from 2021 to 2025 (139→178). Officer overrides have increased 164% over the same period (11→29 per year). Both trends are accelerating. The reform is urgent.
  • Regional variation: The South East has the most committee refusals (200), followed by the East Midlands (107) and East of England (97). London, despite the highest appeal volumes, has just 63 — consistent with higher delegation rates producing better outcomes. The worst LPA override rates: Somerset (43%), North Lincolnshire (38%), Brighton (38%), Durham (36%).
  • The draft guidance has two significant gaps: it contains no provision on training for the nominated member role (despite the PAS finding that 45% of members lack understanding after training), and no reference to the tilted balance under NPPF paragraph 11(d) (despite ~78% of local plans being out of date). These gaps risk undermining the reform’s effectiveness.
5.
We make five principal recommendations, each mapped to a specific provision in the draft Regulations or Guidance:
(i) Amend Regulation 5 to replace the subjective gateway test criteria (Guidance Para 19, Criterion A) with specific, measurable triggers. The current wording — "significant planning matters having regard to the development plan" — is too broad to function as an effective filter.
(ii) Add a new guidance paragraph after Para 19 requiring standardised, evidence-based decision support for gateway test decisions. The draft guidance provides no mechanism for ensuring the quality of the information before the decision-maker.
(iii) Add a new guidance paragraph after Para 14 mandating training as a prerequisite for the nominated member role. The draft guidance is entirely silent on training, despite the PAS finding that 45% of members lack understanding after training.
(iv) Add a new sub-paragraph to Guidance Para 20 addressing out-of-date development plans. The guidance assumes plan compliance can be assessed, but 78% of local plans are out of date. Where the tilted balance (NPPF 11(d)) is engaged, the gateway threshold should be explicitly higher.
(v) Elevate Guidance Para 27 (transparency) into the Regulations. Recording and publishing referral decisions should be a statutory duty, not merely guidance. Mandate annual reporting of delegation rates, appeal outcomes, and costs awards at LPA level.
5A.
To give effect to recommendation (i), we propose the following specific amendment to the draft Regulations:
Proposed amendment to Regulation 5(2):

A Schedule 2 application may be referred to committee only where the nominated officer and nominated member are satisfied that at least one of the following conditions is met:

(a) the application represents a material departure from a detailed site allocation in the adopted development plan, where the departure is material to the principle of development;
(b) a statutory consultee (EA, HE, NE, or LLFA) has raised a holding objection that remains unresolved at the point of determination;
(c) the application has received 50 or more individual representations from separate households raising material planning considerations not addressed by the officer’s report;
(d) the nominated officer certifies in writing that the application raises a novel or unprecedented planning issue not addressed by the development plan, national policy, or established precedent.

Where none of conditions (a)–(d) is met, the application must be delegated.
5B.
The full evidence base, cost-benefit analysis, and regional breakdown supporting these recommendations is set out in Sections 3.6–3.9 below. A companion Evidence Reference Document containing the complete PINS analysis, 500 verified case studies, and detailed annex tables is available alongside this response.

These applications share a common characteristic: the planning issues they raise are capable of resolution through the application of established policy, without the exercise of the kind of broad planning judgment that committee consideration is designed to provide.

The exception for linked listed building consent applications is appropriate. 1.2 Tier B Gateway Test (Schedule 2, Regulation 5, Guidance Paras 19–21) — Needs Stronger Criteria

9.
The gateway test is the centrepiece of the Tier B provisions, and it is here that we have our most significant concern. The current wording — "significant planning matters having regard to the development plan" — is too broad to serve as an effective filter.
10.
To illustrate the scale of the problem: of our 86 case studies, 47 (55%) have environmental, heritage, or landscape constraints that could, under a broad reading, constitute "significant planning matters". These include:
Constraint category Projects affected % of case studies Gateway risk
Conservation area / listed building setting 9 10% High
Flood Zone 2 or 3 17 20% High
AONB / National Landscape 8 9% High
SSSI / SAC / SPA / Ramsar impact zone 7 8% High
Outside settlement boundary / development limits 6 7% High
Compound constraints (2+ of above) 12 14% Very high
11.
The risk is clear. If the gateway test threshold is left subjective, the nominated member has a defensible basis for referring the majority of non-trivial applications to committee. The result would be that the reform achieves the delegation of only the simplest applications (Tier A) while failing to materially reduce committee workload for the cases that consume most time and resources.
Amend Regulation 5 and Guidance Para 19 (Criterion A): Replace the subjective "significant planning matters" criterion with specific, measurable triggers: (a) material departure from a development plan allocation; (b) unresolved holding objection from a statutory consultee (EA, HE, NE, LLFA); (c) 50+ individual representations raising material planning considerations; (d) nominated officer certification of a novel planning issue. Applications not meeting at least one trigger must be delegated. Guidance Para 21 examples (community shop, notable listed building) should be tightened — they currently set such a low threshold that Criterion B could capture the majority of non-trivial applications.
12.
This approach would align with the LGA's position that delegation should be the norm and committee the exception, while providing the clarity that the RTPI has called for in its response to the consultation.
12A.
We recognise the LGA's concern that a national delegation scheme may erode the role of elected councillors as "the backbone of the English planning system". This concern has democratic legitimacy. However, our analysis of 30,000 decision letters demonstrates that democratic oversight is most effective when focused on genuinely complex cases. Of the 749 committee refusals we identified among overturned appeals, 89.7% of all identifiable decision routes were committee decisions. In the 87 cases where committees overrode their own officer, the inspector agreed with the officer every time. Democratic accountability is not served by decisions that are systematically overturned at public expense. The gateway test, with specific measurable triggers, ensures committees see the cases that genuinely benefit from democratic scrutiny — and delegates the rest to qualified professionals.

1.3 The Delegation Default (Regulation 5, Guidance Para 17) — Correct But Needs Enforcement

13.
We support the principle that where the nominated officer and the nominated member disagree on whether the gateway test is met, the application is delegated. This is the correct default — it places the burden of justification on referral rather than delegation.
14.
However, we note a practical concern. In politically sensitive cases, the pressure on nominated members to refer applications to committee will be intense. Members who exercise their role conscientiously by declining to refer may face criticism from colleagues and constituents. The transparency requirement — publishing referral decisions with reasons — is critical and we welcome it.
14A.
We endorse the RTPI's call for a statutory Chief Planning Officer role. Guidance Para 14(a) describes the nominated officer as the "Chief Planning Officer or equivalent officer who has extensive professional planning experience", but this is guidance, not statute. Making the CPO a statutory role — as the RTPI proposes — would give the gateway test decision professional weight, legal standing, and clear accountability. It would also address the LGA's concern about pressure on officers: a statutory CPO has a defined professional duty that provides protection against political pressure to refer inappropriately.
Elevate Guidance Para 27 into Regulations (new Regulation 8): The current requirement to record and publish referral decisions is in guidance only. This should be a statutory duty. Require mandatory annual reporting of: delegation rates, gateway test trigger frequencies, appeal outcomes by decision route (committee vs delegated), and costs awards. Publish centrally so the reform's effectiveness can be monitored. Our PINS analysis identifies LPAs with costs rates of 13.6% (South Somerset) and 11.0% (Cornwall) — this data should be transparent.

1.4 Committee Size Cap (Regulation 7, Guidance Para 28) — Supported

15.
We support the cap of 13 members for planning committees. Smaller committees tend to produce more focused, better-informed debate. The evidence from our case study analysis is that the quality of committee decision-making correlates more strongly with the quality of information before the committee than with its size.
Add new guidance paragraph after Para 14 (Nominated Member): The draft guidance is entirely silent on training. Given the PAS survey finding that 45% of committee members lack a good understanding of planning after training, the guidance should require as a minimum that nominated members have completed accredited planning training within the preceding 12 months. If mandatory training is deferred from the Regulations, the guidance should at least set an expectation that LPAs publish whether their nominated member has completed training.

16.
The original proposal to delegate all reserved matters applications was too sweeping, and we welcome the revised position that the gateway test should apply to phased RMAs. Our data demonstrates why.
17.
Large phased schemes in our case study dataset span considerable timeframes and can evolve materially from the original outline consent:

Later phases of schemes of this scale may incorporate different house types, layouts, densities, and infrastructure solutions from those envisaged at outline stage. National policy may have changed, local plan policies may have been updated, and community expectations may have evolved. The gateway test is an appropriate mechanism for determining whether these differences merit committee scrutiny.

2.2 Size Thresholds

19.
We recommend a size threshold to differentiate the treatment of RMAs:
Proposed threshold:
• RMAs for outline permissions of 150+ dwellings: Tier B (gateway test applies)
• RMAs for outline permissions of fewer than 150 dwellings: Tier A (delegated), on the basis that the outline consent has already received committee scrutiny
20.
Our rationale derives from portfolio analysis. Of the projects in our dataset involving reserved matters, those below 150 units rarely trigger the level of complexity — multi-phase delivery, evolving design codes, changing infrastructure requirements — that justifies re-examination at committee level. The outline consent has already been the subject of committee deliberation; the reserved matters stage is concerned with the detailed execution of an established principle.

2.3 The Missing Piece — Evidence-Based Reserved Matters Assessment

21.
Reserved matters applications deal with appearance, landscaping, layout, scale, and access. These are precisely the areas where data-driven assessment adds the most value: sight lines can be calculated from topographic data; overlooking distances can be derived from building footprints and heights; density can be benchmarked against local character; landscape impact can be modelled from terrain and existing vegetation data.
Add to Guidance Para 24 (Phased Reserved Matters): We support the placement of phased RMAs in Schedule 2 with the strong expectation of delegation. However, the guidance should go further: given that reserved matters (appearance, landscaping, layout, scale, access) are inherently technical and capable of standardised assessment, the guidance should encourage LPAs to adopt evidence-based assessment tools for these decisions. This would strengthen the case for delegation by demonstrating that officer decisions are supported by rigorous, data-driven evaluation.

Section 3: Response to Question 3 — Comments on the Draft Guidance

3.1 Plan Compliance as the Default Filter (Guidance Para 20) — The 23% Problem

22.
The draft guidance positions plan compliance as the primary basis for the gateway test. This assumes that plan compliance can be readily and consistently determined. In our experience, it frequently cannot.
23.
According to Lichfields research, only ~22% of local plans are projected to be up-to-date (adopted within five years) by end 2025, down from 33% in July 2023 ("Timed Out", Lichfields, July 2023). For the remaining ~78%, the weight to be afforded to development plan policies varies, and the interaction between plan policies and national policy (particularly where the tilted balance is engaged) introduces precisely the kind of uncertainty that makes the "significant planning matters" criterion unworkable.

24.
In practice, this means that for the majority of English LPAs, the gateway test will be applied against a development plan whose policies may carry reduced weight. The guidance needs to address this explicitly.
Add new sub-paragraph to Guidance Para 20: Para 20 assumes plan compliance can be readily assessed, but ~78% of local plans are out of date (Lichfields, 2023). The guidance should add: "Where the development plan is more than five years old, or where the local planning authority cannot demonstrate a five-year housing land supply such that the presumption under NPPF paragraph 11(d) is engaged, the threshold for referral under Criterion A should be correspondingly higher. Applications that are policy-compliant under the tilted balance should not be referred to committee unless Criterion B is also met."

3.2 Training and Decision Support (Gap — no provision in draft guidance)

25.
The PAS Modernising Planning Committees National Survey (December 2024, covering 120+ councils — over a third of all councils in England) found that 45% of respondents answered "No" when asked whether training was effective and whether all council members understood planning; only 17% answered "Yes". This is a significant data point. It suggests that training alone is insufficient to ensure the quality of committee decision-making.
26.
Mandatory training was dropped from these regulations. We regard this as a missed opportunity, but recognise the political and practical constraints. However, the gap left by the absence of mandatory training can be partially addressed through investment in decision support tools.
27.
Our analysis across 86 case studies demonstrates that the technical domains requiring specialist knowledge are substantial. Retrospective expert review consistently identifies issues in:
Domain Typical issues found Specialist knowledge required
NPPF paragraph cross-referencing Incorrect paragraph citations (Dec 2024 renumbering) Policy mapping expertise
Flood risk assessment Sequential test applied incorrectly; SFRA/EA data conflicts Hydrology, EA methodology
Heritage significance s.66/s.72 duties not properly structured in balancing exercise Conservation, case law
Viability arithmetic GDV calculation errors (e.g., £268k error found in Sidford, East Devon) Valuation, development finance
BNG metric Habitat baseline miscalculated; metric version discrepancies Ecology, DEFRA metric
Transport assessment Trip generation rates from superseded TRICS data Highway engineering
28.
Committee members cannot reasonably be expected to evaluate these technical domains through training alone. Evidence-based decision support, providing structured briefings on the key technical issues for each application, would materially improve decision quality.
Add new guidance paragraph after Para 19 (Gateway Test): The gateway test decision under Regulation 5 is only as good as the evidence informing it. The guidance should require that the nominated officer’s consideration of whether to refer is supported by a standardised evidence base covering: constraint detection, policy compliance analysis, technical domain assessment (flood, heritage, landscape, ecology, viability, transport), and cross-domain interactions. This is the mechanism by which the gap left by the absence of mandatory training can be partially addressed.

3.3 The Tilted Balance Interaction (Gap — not addressed in draft guidance)

29.
Six LPAs in our dataset cannot demonstrate a five-year housing land supply, engaging the presumption in favour of sustainable development under NPPF paragraph 11(d). The tilted balance is precisely the scenario where committee over-referral and refusal against officer recommendation causes the greatest harm to the planning system.
30.
This is not an isolated concern. Lichfields' research ("Refused for Good Reason?", 2018) analysed all appeals for schemes of 50+ homes and found that 65% of appeals succeeded where the committee had refused against the officer's recommendation, compared with 40% where the refusal followed the officer's recommendation. Site Intelligence's primary analysis of the PINS Casework Database (114,700 appeals, 2015–2025) confirms a 40.9% allowed rate for major residential appeals versus 23.1% for minor. The disparity is significant and points to a systemic issue with committee refusals in the major applications cohort.
30A.
Our case study programme includes documented cases where committees refused applications against the officer's recommendation. Key examples:
Ref LPA Scheme Committee vote Appeal outcome Costs
Bolton-le-Sands (Lancaster CC) Lancaster 200 dwellings — Grey Belt 10–3 against officer rec Appeal pending (refused Feb 2026) HIGH costs risk — single refusal reason
High Barnet Station (TfL/Barratt) Barnet 283 dwellings — station car park 8–1 against officer rec Appeal pending —
APP/Q1445/W/24/3353409 Brighton & Hove Gasworks — 495 homes Refused by committee against officer rec Allowed (Secretary of State) Full costs awarded
31.
The Brighton Gasworks case (APP/Q1445/W/24/3353409) is particularly instructive. The inspector found that the council's reasons for refusal contained "vague, generalised or inaccurate" assertions and awarded full costs — meaning the council bore the applicant's costs as well as its own. This is precisely the outcome that data-driven decision support can help to prevent.
The cost of getting it wrong: Full costs awards require the council to pay both its own costs and the appellant's. For a scheme of 495 homes, total appeal costs (officer time, legal representation, expert witnesses, appellant's costs) can exceed £250,000. This is public money that could instead fund the implementation of decision support tools across the entire authority.
Address the gap between Guidance Para 20 and NPPF paragraph 11(d): The draft guidance does not reference the tilted balance anywhere. This is a significant omission. Our PINS analysis shows a 40.9% overturn rate for major residential appeals nationally — and this rate is higher in LPAs without a five-year supply. Where the tilted balance is engaged, the "significant planning matters" criterion under Regulation 5 should be explicitly calibrated: policy-compliant applications under the tilted balance should require exceptional circumstances for committee referral, because the consequences of a poorly evidenced refusal are more severe (costs exposure, housing delivery delay, inspector scrutiny of the authority’s evidence base).

3.4 Cross-Policy Interactions

32.
The guidance does not adequately address how the gateway test interacts with the cross-policy considerations that characterise most non-trivial planning applications. Our case study data demonstrates the complexity:

Heritage: s.66 / s.72 Statutory Duties

33.
Heritage decisions require a specific balancing exercise that committees frequently misapply. In Falconers, Somerset (Falconers), our automated constraint detection identified heritage constraints that the manual assessment had failed to flag at the appropriate significance level. The divergence between automated and manual assessment illustrates why structured evidence is essential. The statutory duty to have "special regard" (s.66) or "pay special attention" (s.72) requires more than a general assertion that heritage has been considered.

Flood Risk: Sequential and Exception Tests

34.
In Sidford, East Devon (Sidford), the site falls within Flood Zone 3 and within an AONB, creating a compound constraint that requires sequential and exception testing alongside the major development test under NPPF paragraph 190. In a case study site, the EA flood zones and the LPA's own SFRA data conflicted — a technical issue that requires specialist knowledge to resolve. The sequential test in particular involves a comparative site search that is methodical and data-intensive, not a matter of broad planning judgment.

AONB / National Landscape: Major Development Test

35.
Major development in a National Landscape requires a weighing of public interest under NPPF paragraph 190 (formerly 177). This is a structured test with defined criteria, not a matter for general committee debate. Our Sidford, East Devon analysis demonstrates that the test can be applied systematically using landscape sensitivity data, economic impact assessment, and alternative site analysis.

Biodiversity Net Gain

36.
Mandatory BNG since February 2024 requires metric calculations that cannot be performed in a committee setting. The DEFRA statutory metric involves baseline habitat classification, condition assessment, and mathematical calculation of biodiversity units. This is a technical exercise that produces a numerical output. Committee members can assess whether the outcome is acceptable, but cannot meaningfully scrutinise the calculation itself without specialist support.

Viability

37.
In Sidford, East Devon, our expert review identified a £268,000 error in the Gross Development Value arithmetic that would have affected the committee's assessment of the affordable housing contribution. Viability assessments involve residual land value calculations, construction cost benchmarking, and profit margin assumptions that require development finance expertise. Without structured, data-backed analysis, committee members have no basis for evaluating viability arguments.
Add to Guidance Para 19 (gateway test application): The guidance should recognise that cross-policy interactions (s.66/s.72 heritage duties, sequential/exception flood tests, NPPF para 190 landscape tests, BNG metric calculations, viability assessments) are inherently technical and do not constitute "significant planning matters" merely because they are complex. These are structured exercises with defined methodologies — precisely the kind of assessment that benefits from evidence-based decision support rather than committee debate. The gateway test decision should be informed by a standardised analysis of these interactions, and Criterion A should not be triggered solely by the presence of a technical constraint.

Stevenage, Arun, Chorley, Portsmouth, Newcastle-under-Lyme, Hyndburn, and Bolsover are all noted in our LPA profiles as having faced housing delivery challenges and housing land supply shortfalls.

Committees that refuse applications in these circumstances are more likely to have their decisions overturned — not because inspectors are more permissive, but because the policy framework already presumes in favour of granting permission. 3.7 Proposed Regulation Amendments

37R.
Having analysed 114,700 appeals across a full decade and the draft Regulations (particularly Regulation 5 and Schedules 1 and 2), we offer specific drafting proposals for the gateway test. The current wording of Regulation 5 permits referral where the application "raises a significant planning matter having regard to the development plan and other material considerations" or "raises an economic, social or environmental issue of significance to the local area". These two limbs are too broad. We propose the following amendments:
Proposed amendment to Regulation 5(2):

A Schedule 2 application may be referred to committee only where the nominated officer and nominated member are satisfied that at least one of the following conditions is met:

(a) the application represents a material departure from a detailed site allocation in the adopted development plan, where the departure is material to the principle of development (not merely to matters of design, layout, or landscaping);

(b) a statutory consultee (the Environment Agency, Historic England, Natural England, or the Lead Local Flood Authority) has raised a holding objection that remains unresolved at the point of determination;

(c) the application has received 50 or more individual representations from separate households within the administrative area of the LPA, raising material planning considerations that are not addressed by the officer’s report;

(d) the nominated officer certifies in writing that the application raises a novel or unprecedented planning issue not addressed by the development plan, national policy, or established appeal precedent.

Where none of conditions (a)–(d) is met, the application must be delegated.
37S.
This formulation achieves three objectives. First, it replaces subjective criteria with measurable triggers. Second, it preserves democratic accountability for genuinely contentious cases (conditions (c) and (d)). Third, it prevents the gateway test from being used as a back door for political referral — the pattern that our PINS analysis shows produces the highest costs exposure for LPAs.
37T.
We note that the draft guidance already states that applications broadly complying with a detailed site allocation should not be considered to raise significant planning matters. Our proposed condition (a) codifies this principle in the Regulations themselves, giving it statutory force rather than leaving it as guidance that can be disregarded.

Section 4: The Role of Technology in Supporting the Reform

38.
The gateway test would benefit from a standardised evidence base that both the nominated officer and the nominated member can reference. The planning system already generates the data needed — constraint registers, flood maps, heritage designations, housing land supply figures, appeal decisions, and policy provisions. The challenge is not availability but synthesis: presenting this data in an accessible, structured format for decision-makers. Technology platforms already exist that can provide automated constraint detection, policy compliance analysis, and cross-domain impact assessment for individual applications. Encouraging LPAs to adopt such tools would reduce the reliance on subjective judgment and produce the kind of specific, evidenced reasoning that withstands appeal scrutiny.
39.
The Brighton Gasworks case (APP/Q1445/W/24/3353409) illustrates the cost of the current approach. The inspector found the committee’s reasons for refusal were “vague, generalised or inaccurate” and awarded full costs. Had the committee been provided with a structured analysis of the site’s constraint profile and policy compliance position — all derivable from publicly available data — the refusal would either not have occurred or would have been defensible at appeal. The £66m–£166m costs burden identified in our PINS analysis (Section 3.6) is, in substantial part, a failure of evidence rather than a failure of judgment.
40.
We recommend that the guidance encourage LPAs to adopt data-driven decision support tools as part of the gateway test process, and that MHCLG’s mandatory annual reporting of delegation rates include appeal success rates by decision route (delegated vs committee). This would create a feedback loop that demonstrates the reform’s effectiveness and identifies authorities requiring targeted support. The monitoring should specify a responsible body, an intervention threshold, and a mechanism for addressing persistent high overturn rates.

A Note on Democratic Accountability

50.
We recognise that planning committee reform raises legitimate concerns about democratic accountability. Elected members have a democratic mandate to represent their communities, and the planning process is one of the most visible ways in which that mandate is exercised. Reducing the scope for committee involvement risks being perceived as diminishing local democratic control.
51.
We would argue that the opposite is true. Poorly evidenced committee refusals that are subsequently overturned on appeal — often with costs awarded against the council — do not serve democratic accountability. They expose councils to financial liability, delay housing delivery, and undermine public confidence in the planning system. Better evidence produces better democratic outcomes: committees that are well-informed, well-trained, and equipped with structured analysis of the issues before them will make decisions that are more defensible, more consistent, and more likely to reflect the genuine planning merits of a proposal. The reform does not remove democratic oversight — it concentrates it where it adds most value.
