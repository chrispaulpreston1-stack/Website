Planning Committee Reform: Explained

What the data tells us, why it matters, and what you can do about it. Based on the largest independent analysis of planning appeal decisions in England.

Why this guide exists

The government has published draft Regulations and Guidance to reform how planning committees work. The consultation closes on 23 April 2026. The reforms will change which applications go to committee and which are decided by officers.

Site Intelligence has parsed 188,933 official PINS records, analysed 114,700 planning appeals over 10 years, and classified 30,000 decision letters to understand what's actually happening when committees make decisions. This guide explains the findings in plain English.

No jargon. No assumptions about what you already know. Just the data and what it means.

1

What happens when a council refuses a planning application?

And how often are they wrong?

When a local planning authority (council) refuses a planning application, the applicant can appeal to an independent inspector appointed by the Planning Inspectorate (PINS). The inspector reviews the evidence and makes a decision: either allow the appeal (overturn the refusal) or dismiss it (uphold the refusal).

Over the last 10 years, inspectors have overturned 28.5% of all refusals. That means roughly 1 in 3.5 refusals was wrong — the inspector found the council didn't have sufficient grounds to refuse.

The headline numbers (2015–2025)
Total planning appeals decided114,700
Appeals allowed (refusal overturned)32,687 (28.5%)
Appeals dismissed (refusal upheld)79,175
Costs applied for by appellants6,108

But those averages hide a much bigger problem with major development...

If you're a councillor

1 in 3.5 of your council's refusals are being overturned. Each one costs time, money, and reputation. Ask your planning officer for your council's specific overturn rate.

If you're a planning officer

Use these national benchmarks to calibrate your recommendations. If your authority's rate is above 28.5%, there's a systemic issue worth investigating.

If you're a developer

Check your council's overturn rate before deciding whether to appeal. Some councils lose nearly half their appeals — appeal may be a realistic strategy.

Why are major developments refused more than they should be?

The 40.9% problem

Here's the most important statistic: 40.9% of all refused major residential applications (10+ homes) are overturned on appeal. That's nearly double the 23.1% rate for smaller developments.

This means that for every 10 major housing schemes a council refuses, 4 are overturned by an inspector. Those 4 schemes were delayed — sometimes by years — causing real harm: homes not built, costs incurred, communities waiting.

Why the gap? Major applications are more likely to go to planning committee rather than being decided by a professional planning officer. And that's where the problem lies.

What does this mean in practice?

Over 10 years, 2,247 major housing schemes were refused by councils and then allowed by inspectors. Each appeal takes months. Each delays homes. The reform is designed to reduce this number by ensuring only genuinely complex applications go to committee.

What happens when committees ignore their own officers?

The officer override problem

Planning officers are qualified professionals who assess applications against policy and evidence. They make a recommendation to the committee: approve or refuse. The committee can follow the recommendation or override it.

We analysed 30,000 appeal decision letters — covering 91.8% of all allowed appeals in the 10-year dataset. From the text of each letter, we identified whether the original refusal was made by committee or under delegated authority, and whether the committee went against the officer's recommendation.

Decision letter classification results
Decision letters analysed30,000
Committee decisions identified749
Committee refused against officer recommendation87
Committee decisions as % of identifiable routes89.7%
Delegated decisions identified56

That 89.7% figure is striking. Among appeals that were overturned, committee refusals outnumber delegated refusals by roughly 9 to 1 where the decision route is identifiable. This doesn't mean committees are always wrong — but it does mean that when a refusal is overturned, it was overwhelmingly likely to have been a committee decision.

In all 87 cases where we could identify that the committee went against their officer's recommendation, the inspector sided with the officer. Independent research by Lichfields confirms this pattern nationally: 65% of all appeals succeed where committees refuse against officer recommendation, compared with 40% where the refusal followed the officer's advice.

What does "against officer recommendation" mean?

The planning officer's report recommended that the application be approved. The committee voted to refuse it anyway. The applicant appealed. The inspector reviewed the evidence and agreed with the officer — the application should have been approved. In many cases, the inspector awarded costs against the council, meaning the taxpayer paid for both sides.

If you're a councillor

When you override your officer, you're betting the council's money on being right. The data shows that bet loses far more often than it wins. Make sure your reasons are specific, evidenced, and defensible at appeal.

If you're a planning officer

If your recommendation is overridden, document the officer's report thoroughly. The appeal inspector will read it. Clear, evidence-based reports protect both you and the authority.

If you're a developer

If your application is refused against officer recommendation, appeal prospects are strong. The Lichfields data shows 65% success rate. Ensure your appeal evidence mirrors the officer's reasoning.

What does a costs award actually cost the taxpayer?

The hidden bill

When an inspector finds that a council behaved "unreasonably" in refusing an application, they can award costs. This means the council has to pay not just its own costs, but the applicant's costs too — legal fees, consultant fees, expert witnesses, the lot.

Over 10 years, costs were applied for in 6,108 planning appeals. Of those, 2,670 were in appeals that were allowed — meaning the inspector found both that the refusal was wrong and that the council's behaviour was unreasonable.

Estimated total taxpayer burden (10 years)£66m – £166m

That's between £66 million and £166 million of public money spent defending refusals that inspectors found to be unjustified. To put that in context: it's enough to fund hundreds of planning officer posts, or invest in the evidence-based decision support tools that could prevent these refusals in the first place.

Which councils have the worst costs exposure?

Cornwall Council tops the list with 87 costs awards in allowed appeals over 10 years. Wiltshire (43), Barnet (44), Bromley (36), and Cheshire East (33) follow. South Somerset has the highest costs rate at 13.6% — meaning 1 in 7 of their appeals result in costs against the council.

Do site constraints justify committee refusal?

Green Belt, heritage, flooding — when are they genuine reasons to refuse?

Many committee refusals cite site constraints — Green Belt, listed building settings, flood risk, AONB/National Landscape, ecological designations. These are legitimate planning considerations. But the data shows that inspectors frequently find the evidence doesn't support refusal, even where constraints exist.

Allowed appeals in constrained locations (10 years)
Green Belt3,046 allowed
Listed building / conservation area733 allowed
AONB / National Landscape293 allowed
Flood risk90 allowed
SSSI / SAC / SPA64 allowed
Total constrained allowed4,056

3,046 Green Belt appeals allowed. That doesn't mean Green Belt policy is wrong — it means councils are applying it inconsistently. In many cases, the inspector found that "Very Special Circumstances" existed, or that the development was not inappropriate, or that the harm to openness was outweighed by the benefits.

The key point for the reform: the presence of a constraint should not automatically trigger committee referral. Constraints are technical matters with defined assessment methodologies (sequential test, heritage balance, landscape character assessment). They benefit from structured, evidence-based analysis — exactly what professional officers provide.

If you're a councillor

A constraint is not an automatic reason to refuse. Ask your officer: "What does the structured assessment show?" If the officer's report says the tests are met, you need strong, specific evidence to disagree.

If you're a planning officer

When recommending approval on a constrained site, make the structured assessment explicit. Show the sequential test, the heritage balance, the landscape assessment. Give the committee the evidence to make an informed decision.

If you're a developer

If your site has constraints, invest in robust technical evidence upfront. The data shows inspectors allow constrained applications when the evidence is thorough. Front-load the technical work.

What is the gateway test and why does it matter?

The centrepiece of the reform

Under the proposed reforms, planning applications are split into two tiers:

Tier A (Schedule 1) — householder applications, minor development, discharge of conditions, lawful development certificates. These must be delegated to officers in all circumstances. The committee never sees them.

Tier B (Schedule 2) — everything else, including major residential. These can go to committee, but only if they pass the gateway test.

The gateway test is the filter. It asks: does this application raise "significant planning matters having regard to the development plan" or "economic, social or environmental significance to the local area"? If yes, it goes to committee. If no, the officer decides.

The problem with the current wording

The terms "significant planning matters" and "significance to the local area" are subjective. The draft guidance gives examples including "a change of use of a community shop in a rural area" — a threshold so low that almost any non-trivial application could qualify. Without tighter criteria, the gateway test risks becoming a rubber stamp for referral rather than a genuine filter.

Site Intelligence's analysis of 114,700 appeals suggests the test needs specific, measurable triggers. We've proposed four:

Proposed gateway test triggers (amendment to Regulation 5)
(a) Material departure from a development plan allocationObjective test
(b) Unresolved statutory consultee objection (EA, HE, NE, LLFA)Objective test
(c) 50+ individual representations raising material considerationsMeasurable threshold
(d) Officer certifies a novel planning issueProfessional judgment

If none of these conditions is met, the application must be delegated. This gives committees the cases that genuinely need democratic oversight, while ensuring that routine applications — even large ones — are decided efficiently by qualified professionals.

What about out-of-date local plans?

The 78% problem

The gateway test assumes you can assess whether an application complies with the development plan. But what if the plan is out of date?

According to Lichfields research, only about 22% of local plans in England are up to date (adopted within the last five years). The remaining 78% are older, and their policies carry reduced weight under national planning policy.

When a council can't demonstrate a five-year housing land supply, the "tilted balance" under NPPF paragraph 11(d) kicks in. This means there's a presumption in favour of granting permission unless the harm significantly outweighs the benefits. Committees that refuse applications in these circumstances are more likely to be overturned.

The gap in the draft guidance

The draft guidance does not mention the tilted balance anywhere. It doesn't address what happens when the development plan is out of date. This is a significant omission — for the majority of English councils, the gateway test will be applied against a policy base of uncertain weight. The guidance needs explicit provision for this.

Does training help?

What the PAS survey found

The Planning Advisory Service (PAS) surveyed 120+ councils in December 2024 about planning committee effectiveness. The result: 45% of respondents said committee members did not have a good understanding of planning after training. Only 17% said "yes".

The draft guidance is entirely silent on training. There's no requirement for the nominated member (the person who decides whether to refer applications to committee) to have completed any training at all.

This matters because the gateway test is only as good as the person applying it. If nearly half of members don't understand planning after training, the quality of gateway test decisions will be variable — and the reform's effectiveness will be undermined.

Our recommendation

Add a new guidance paragraph requiring that nominated members have completed accredited planning training within the preceding 12 months. This isn't about excluding people — it's about ensuring that the person making the gateway test decision has the knowledge to make it well.

Making it personal

Every council in England has a different appeal overturn rate. Some are well below the national average. Others are consistently above 40%. Your council's rate tells you something important about the quality of planning decisions being made in your area.
