1. Introduction & Site Description

Falconers is a Grade II listed manor house (HE 1289162) set in approximately 7.2 acres on Moult Hill, Salcombe. The site comprises two separately registered freehold titles: DN170405 (the listed house, formal gardens, and outbuildings — 4.22 acres) and DN263903 (paddock and open land to the west — 2.98 acres).

Constructed in 1925 in Kentish Wealden style using hand-crafted English oak for the British Empire Exhibition at Wembley, Falconers was subsequently dismantled and reassembled on this elevated site above Salcombe. The house contains significant architectural features including a full-height great hall with central timber truss, stained glass windows, and a galleried landing.

The site falls within the South Devon National Landscape (formerly AONB), is likely within the Salcombe Conservation Area, and is situated within a heritage-rich cluster including the Grade II* Moult, Grade II Former Lodge to The Moult (HE 1213029), and Grade II Moult Summerhouse (HE 1213027).

3. Proposal 1 — Outbuilding Conversion (DN170405)

3.1 Policy Justification

NPPF 84(c) permits the development of isolated homes in the countryside where the proposal involves "the re-use of redundant or disused buildings and would lead to an enhancement of the immediate setting."

Falconers contains detached outbuildings including a double garage and ancillary structures. Where these buildings are structurally capable of conversion without substantial reconstruction, their re-use as residential accommodation is supported by national policy, provided the conversion enhances the immediate setting.

4. Proposal 2 — Exceptional Dwelling (DN263903)

4.1 Policy Justification

NPPF 84(e) permits isolated homes in the countryside where the design is "truly outstanding, reflecting the highest standards in architecture, and would help to raise standards of design more generally in rural areas" and "would significantly enhance its immediate setting, and be sensitive to the defining characteristics of the local area."

This policy route has delivered approximately 150 dwellings nationally since 1997, including 11 within National Landscapes and multiple within National Parks. The bar is deliberately high, but the economic fundamentals of Salcombe make it viable: achievable values of £2.5–4m justify the £500k+ investment in architectural excellence needed to clear the "truly outstanding" test.

4.2 The Two Limbs of 84(e)

Limb 1: Architectural Excellence The design must be truly outstanding, reflecting the highest standards in architecture, and would help raise design standards more generally in rural areas. Note: the 2018 NPPF revision removed the former requirement for designs to be "innovative" or "groundbreaking." Relatively modest, beautifully crafted homes can now qualify. The key is sustained excellence in concept, detail, materiality, and environmental performance.
Limb 2: Setting Enhancement The design must significantly enhance its immediate setting AND be sensitive to the defining characteristics of the local area. This is the more commonly failed test. Inspector decisions consistently emphasise that "change" is not the same as "enhancement" — the development must demonstrably improve the landscape quality of the site, not merely coexist with it.

4.4 Why This Site Suits 84(e)

  • Functional paddock, not pristine landscape: The current setting is a mown paddock with a helipad — functional land with limited landscape value. Almost any well-designed development would enhance it through considered planting, landscaping, and architecture.
  • Separate title reduces heritage friction: DN263903 is not the designed gardens of a listed building. The curtilage argument (see Heritage Impact tab) strongly favours exclusion, meaning development faces a setting-impact test, not a direct LBC requirement.
  • Values justify the architectural investment: A 3,000–4,000 sq ft home on Moult Hill achieves £2.5–4m. This supports £500k+ in architectural fees and design review costs — the economics work.
  • Principal residence addresses local need: Salcombe's 57% second-home rate is the planning issue of the day. A permanently occupied, architect-designed family home is the antithesis of absentee ownership.
  • Successful precedent in designated landscapes: Nithurst Farm (South Downs NP), Lake House (High Weald AONB), Coach House (South Downs NP), Water Farm (Dedham Vale AONB) all demonstrate that 84(e) is achievable in protected landscapes.

2. Curtilage Analysis — DN263903 (Paddock)

The critical question for Proposal 2 is whether the 2.98-acre paddock (DN263903) falls within the curtilage of the listed building. If it does, any structures on the land pre-dating 1 July 1948 are also listed, and Listed Building Consent would be required for development affecting the character of the curtilage. If it does not, development on DN263903 must still respect the setting of Falconers but is not subject to direct LBC requirements for the land itself.

2.1 The Three Calderdale Tests

Calderdale [1982] QB 1019 established the three-part test for curtilage, refined by Debenhams [1987] AC 396, Dyer v Dorset [1989], and Egerton v Taunton Deane [2008] EWHC 2752. Historic England Advice Note 10 (updated June 2025) provides the current guidance. All factors are assessed at the date of listing.

TestAnalysisFinding
1. Physical Layout DN263903 sits to the west/northwest of Falconers, separated from the designed gardens by boundary features. The paddock is open grassland with a helipad — physically and visually distinct from the intimate, planted gardens around the house. In Dyer v Dorset, the court held that curtilage extends to "the stables and other outbuildings, the gardens and the rough grass up to the ha-ha" but does NOT include the wider country park. The paddock at Falconers is analogous to the country park, not the domestic garden. OUTSIDE
2. Ownership (Historic & Current) DN263903 is held under a separate registered title with a separate ownership history. In Egerton v Taunton Deane, Justice Sullivan held that a farmhouse was "physically separated from, and functionally distinct from, the agricultural land" even under common ownership. Where separate titles exist, this is strong (though not conclusive) evidence of distinct curtilages. Planning inspectors have cited separate Land Registry titles as supporting evidence for exclusion from curtilage. OUTSIDE
3. Use/Function (Historic & Current) The paddock has been in agricultural/grazing use, not ancillary domestic use. In Debenhams, the House of Lords emphasised that curtilage requires an "ancillary" functional relationship. In Egerton, agricultural buildings in agricultural use were held NOT to be curtilage-listed despite common ownership with the listed farmhouse. A paddock used for grazing or a helipad is functional land serving a distinct purpose, not ancillary to the domestic occupation of Falconers. OUTSIDE
Conclusion: DN263903 Is Likely Outside the Curtilage On all three Calderdale tests — physical layout, ownership history, and functional use — the paddock land is more likely than not to fall outside the curtilage of the listed building. This is consistent with Dyer (country estate paddocks beyond the ha-ha), Egerton (agricultural land functionally distinct from farmhouse), and Historic England Advice Note 10 (self-contained land with separate curtilage). We recommend seeking formal confirmation from the LPA as part of this pre-application process.

1. South Devon National Landscape

The site falls within the South Devon National Landscape (redesignated from AONB in Autumn 2023 under the Levelling-up and Regeneration Act 2023). Section 245 of LURA 2023 places a statutory duty on the LPA to "seek to further the statutory purposes" of National Landscapes — a stronger duty than the previous CROW Act s.85 "have regard to."

1.1 Is This "Major Development"?

NPPF 190 states that major development in a National Landscape should be refused unless exceptional circumstances apply. However, "major development" in this context is not defined by the standard 10-unit threshold. It is a matter of fact and degree, considering the nature, scale, setting, and potential significant adverse impact.

Our Position: Neither Proposal Constitutes Major Development
  • Proposal 1: Conversion of existing outbuildings with no new footprint, no change to massing, no landscape impact. This is unambiguously not major development.
  • Proposal 2: A single dwelling on 2.98 acres, carefully sited within existing boundary planting. Inspectors have consistently found that a single dwelling does not constitute major development in an AONB (see Sunny Ridge APP/K1128/W/22/3301425, allowed in Salcombe). The NPPF 190 test is not engaged.

1.2 LVIA Scope

A full Landscape and Visual Impact Assessment will accompany the planning application for Proposal 2. The scope should address:

  • Viewpoint analysis from: Moult Hill road, North Sands beach, South West Coast Path, Salcombe harbour/waterfront, and key elevated positions
  • Assessment of the paddock's contribution to the landscape character of the National Landscape
  • Landscape enhancement strategy: native woodland planting, hedgerow restoration, biodiversity net gain
  • Seasonal analysis (winter views when deciduous screening is reduced)
  • Night-time impact assessment (lighting is critical for bat habitat)
2. South Hams SAC — Greater Horseshoe Bats

The South Hams Special Area of Conservation is designated for the protection of greater horseshoe bats (Rhinolophus ferrumequinum) — the largest UK population (1,000+ adults, 31% of the national population). The SAC designation triggers Habitats Regulations Assessment requirements for development within Sustenance Zones and Landscape Connectivity Zones.

Action Required
  • Confirm whether Moult Hill falls within a South Hams SAC Sustenance Zone or Landscape Connectivity Zone (check Natural England's consultation zone maps)
  • Commission a Preliminary Ecological Appraisal (PEA) covering both proposals
  • If within a consultation zone, prepare a shadow Habitats Regulations Assessment
  • Lighting design is critical — external lighting must be minimised and bat-friendly (warm white, <2700K, downward-facing, no light spill to boundaries)
  • Existing outbuildings should be assessed for bat roost potential before conversion

3. Landscape Enhancement Strategy

The landscape strategy is as important as the architecture for 84(e). The paddock currently contributes minimal biodiversity or landscape value. The enhancement package should include:

  • Native woodland planting: Mixed native species (oak, ash, hazel, field maple, holly) to extend existing woodland character and provide long-term screening and habitat

  • Hedgerow restoration: Devon bank hedgerows along boundaries — bat foraging corridors and landscape character
  • Orchard: Heritage apple/pear varieties — productive landscape with community/character value
  • Sustainable drainage: Rain gardens, swales, permeable surfaces — surface water management as landscape feature
The Enhancement Argument A helipad on mown grass becomes a wildflower meadow with native woodland, a habitat corridor for horseshoe bats, and a single, exquisitely crafted home. This is not "change" — it is measurable, quantifiable enhancement. The BNG metric, the LVIA, and the ecological assessment will all demonstrate net improvement.
